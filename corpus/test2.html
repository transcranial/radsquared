Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome: Role of Imaging in Diagnosis

Introduction
Section:
prevnext

In 1935, Stein and Leventhal (1) were the first to describe a condition consisting of amenorrhea, obesity, and masculinizing symptoms that is now known as polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS). PCOS has a prevalence of 6.6% in women of reproductive age and affects 4–5 million women of reproductive age in the United States (2). Although this condition is now recognized as the most common endocrine abnormality in women of reproductive age, there is still no universal consensus on diagnostic criteria for PCOS, and its etiology remains incompletely understood.

In this article, we discuss PCOS in terms of health consequences, pathophysiology, and diagnostic criteria. In addition, we discuss imaging and reporting considerations, as well as the clinical evaluation of patients with documented PCOS.

Health Consequences of PCOS
Section:
prevnext

The clinical consequences of PCOS are broad and extend beyond infertility and the reproductive axis.

It is believed that insulin resistance may be central to the etiology of the syndrome (3) and related to many of the health consequences of PCOS (Table 1).
Women with PCOS are at an increased risk for hypertension, hyperlipidemia, type 2 diabetes mellitus, coronary artery disease, and cerebral vascular disease.

A substantial proportion of women with PCOS are overweight. Estimates vary, but approximately 30%–70% of all PCOS patients are clinically obese (body mass index ≥30 kg/m2). In the United States, the mean body mass index in PCOS patients is 35–38 kg/m2 (4). Although obesity itself is not considered the causative event for the development of the syndrome, excess adiposity can exacerbate associated reproductive and metabolic derangements. In particular, increased adiposity is associated with hyperandrogenemia, insulin resistance, glucose intolerance, and dyslipidemia.

With data controlled for age, women with PCOS are more frequently glucose intolerant or diabetic than are their non-PCOS counterparts (5). Impaired glucose tolerance is an independent risk factor for both type 2 diabetes mellitus and cardiovascular disease. Among women with PCOS, the annual rate of conversion from normal glucose tolerance to impaired glucose tolerance is 16% (5). By the 4th decade of life, 33%–40% of women with PCOS will have impaired glucose tolerance and 10% will have type 2 diabetes mellitus. In comparison, among women 20–44 years of age in the United States, 7.8% will have impaired glucose tolerance and 1% will have diabetes (5).

Women with PCOS appear to have greater impairment of endothelial function than can be explained by obesity alone (6), and they may become hypertensive. In addition, women with PCOS tend to have an atherogenic lipid profile, characterized by increased triglycerides, low-density lipoproteins, and very-low-density lipoproteins, as well as decreased high-density lipoproteins. In fact, women with PCOS who undergo coronary computed tomography (CT) have more extensive coronary artery disease than do age-matched control subjects (7) and, in the premenopausal years, have a higher prevalence of subclinical carotid atherosclerosis (8). This increased atherosclerotic burden leads to an increased risk of vascular complications, such as myocardial infarction (9).

Because many of the health consequences of PCOS are related to insulin resistance, it is not surprising that PCOS is related to another syndrome which also has insulin resistance as a central pathogenic feature. Often referred to as “insulin resistance syndrome,” “syndrome X,” or simply “metabolic syndrome,” this syndrome is defined by the National Cholesterol Education Program’s Adult Treatment Panel III as the presence of at least three of the following five conditions: (a) abdominal obesity (waist circumference greater than 35 inches), (b) serum triglyceride level of 150 mg/dL or higher, (c) serum high-density lipoprotein cholesterol level of less than 50 mg/dL, (d) blood pressure of 130/85 mm Hg or higher, and (e) serum fasting glucose level of 100 mg/dL or higher (10). Even in the absence of baseline cardiovascular disease and diabetes, patients with metabolic syndrome are at significantly higher risk for coronary artery disease and all-cause mortality (11). It has been shown that women with PCOS have nearly an 11-fold increased risk for metabolic syndrome compared with age-matched control subjects, and that young women (<30 years old) with PCOS have a significantly higher prevalence of metabolic syndrome (24%) than do both age-matched control subjects (0%) and women participating in the National Health and Nutritional Examination Survey III (6.7%) (12).

Women with PCOS are at a higher risk for endometrial hyperplasia and carcinoma. This is largely thought to be the result of unopposed estrogen associated with anovulatory cycles and the lack of progesterone-induced inhibition of proliferation and differentiation to secretory endometrium following ovulation. The degree of risk for endometrial carcinoma in women with PCOS is still being investigated (13,14). Breast and ovarian cancers have also been variably associated with PCOS (14). Obesity, anovulation, infertility, and hormonal treatment for infertility are seen so frequently in patients with PCOS that it is difficult to isolate PCOS as an independent risk factor for these types of cancer (15).

Pathophysiology of PCOS
Section:
prevnext

One of the most consistent biochemical features of PCOS is the hypersecretion of androgens (16). This results from abnormalities at all levels of the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis (Fig 1). LH regulates the synthesis of androgens from cholesterol in the ovarian theca cells. FSH regulates the aromatase activity of granulosa cells, determining how much estrogen is synthesized from androgenic precursors. When LH increases relative to FSH (ie, when the LH-FSH ratio is elevated), the ovaries preferentially synthesize androgen. The relative proportion of LH and FSH synthesis in the pituitary gland is controlled in the hypothalamus by the GnRH pulse frequency. An increased hypothalamic GnRH pulse frequency favors transcription of the β-subunit of LH over the β-subunit of FSH, thereby increasing the ratio of LH to FSH and favoring the synthesis of androgens. Because women with PCOS usually have an increased LH-FSH ratio, it has been inferred that the GnRH pulse frequency is accelerated in this syndrome (17).

Figure 1 Drawing illustrates the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis in PCOS. Increased androgen production is thought to result from an increased gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) pulse frequency in the hypothalamus, which in turn favors the production of luteinizing hormone (LH) over follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) in the anterior pituitary gland. LH acts on the theca cells in the ovarian stroma to increase the production of androgens. The decreased action of FSH decreases aromatase-mediated conversion of androgens to estrogens in the granulosa cells. 17-OH-progesterone = 17-hydroxyprogesterone.

The role of insulin resistance in PCOS is more complex, since insulin plays both direct and indirect roles in the pathogenesis of hyperandrogenemia (Fig 2). At the level of the ovary, insulin acts synergistically with LH to enhance androgen production of the theca cells. Insulin also inhibits hepatic synthesis of SHBG, increasing the amount of unbound (free) or bioactive testosterone in the circulation and increasing the effect of circulating androgens.

Figure 2 Drawing illustrates the role of insulin resistance in PCOS. Insulin increases the action of LH at the ovary, favoring the production of androgens. In addition, insulin-mediated inhibition of sex hormone–binding globulin (SHBG) synthesis in the liver increases the fraction of free androgens in the serum. Increased adiposity worsens insulin resistance and thus exacerbates the metabolic and endocrine derangements of PCOS.

The pathogenesis of ovulatory dysfunction is less well understood but is also thought to be the result of hyperandrogenism.

Morphologically, the characteristic feature of polycystic ovaries is an apparent failure to select a dominant follicle and the accumulation of antral follicles 2–8 mm in size. It is assumed that this appearance reflects an androgen-induced arrest in antral follicle development (16).

Criteria for PCOS
Section:
prevnext
Evolution of Criteria

Multiple attempts have been made to form a consensus as to the definition of PCOS (Table 2). During the first international conference on PCOS, held at the NIH in 1990, there was little consensus on standard diagnostic criteria for PCOS. Indeed, the lack of clinical trial evidence at that time led to the creation of the following criteria based on majority expert opinion: (a) clinical or biochemical evidence of hyperandrogenism, (b) chronic anovulation, and (c) exclusion of other disorders (18). These criteria spurred a number of large-scale clinical trials (4,19), which in turn led to an awareness that PCOS likely had clinical manifestations broader than those defined by the 1990 NIH criteria (20).

In recognition of the heterogeneous nature of the syndrome, the Rotterdam ESHRE/ASRM–Sponsored PCOS Consensus Workshop Group sought a broader definition for PCOS. This group added the ultrasonographic (US) finding of polycystic ovaries to the diagnostic criteria (18). These 2003 criteria required at least two of the following three conditions to be present for the diagnosis of PCOS: (a) oligo- or anovulation, (b) clinical or biochemical signs of hyperandrogenism, and (c) polycystic ovaries (Fig 3).

The Androgen Excess and PCOS Society issued its own criteria in 2009, describing their work as a modification of the original 1990 NIH criteria (21). These criteria emphasize the role of hyperandrogenism and include the presence of

polycystic ovaries at US. Specifically, these criteria describe patients with PCOS as having both (a) hyperandrogenism (hirsutism or hyperandrogenemia) and (b) ovarian dysfunction (oligo- or anovulation or polycystic ovaries) (21).

What constitutes the best definition of PCOS is still a matter of debate. The Androgen Excess and PCOS Society criteria, although the most recent, should not necessarily be construed as being the most correct. Regardless, the two most recent sets of consensus criteria have established the US finding of polycystic ovaries as part of the diagnostic criteria for PCOS.

Exclusion of Other Disorders

One requirement shared by all sets of criteria for PCOS is the exclusion of other etiologies. Although this does classify PCOS as a diagnosis of exclusion, it does not imply that PCOS is a diagnosis of last resort. Ovulatory Dysfunction

Although infertility is frequently seen in PCOS, a significant proportion of women with PCOS are ovulatory, demonstrating either normal ovulatory patterns or oligomenorrhea (ie, fewer than nine menstrual cycles per year). This has important implications for US evaluation and reporting in PCOS.

US Findings

The goal of imaging in PCOS is to properly identify and document the presence of polycystic ovaries. However, because PCOS is by definition a syndrome, the presence of polycystic ovaries alone is insufficient for diagnosis. The clinical significance of incidentally discovered polycystic ovaries is still unknown, and women with this finding should not be considered to have PCOS unless and until further workup is performed (23).

The 2003 joint ESHRE/ASRM meeting in Rotterdam created a consensus definition for the polycystic ovary, which was reaffirmed by the Androgen Excess and PCOS Society in 2009 (21) and subsequently incorporated into American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists guidelines (22). The definition acknowledges two criteria on the basis of which a polycystic ovary may be identified: ovarian volume and number of follicles. These criteria are based on a review of the literature comparing women with PCOS with healthy control subjects. According to the consensus definition, polycystic ovaries are present when (a) one or both ovaries demonstrate 12 or more follicles measuring 2–9 mm in diameter, or (b) the ovarian volume exceeds 10 cm3. Only one ovary meeting either of these criteria is sufficient to establish the presence of polycystic ovaries (24).

Ovarian Volume.—The question of what constitutes an abnormally large ovarian volume has been the subject of considerable investigation (25–30). This literature compares ovarian volumes in healthy women with those in women with PCOS. A volume of 10 cm3 was selected as the threshold volume for a polycystic ovary (25). It was understood at the time that some studies measuring ovarian volume in unscreened populations suggest a higher threshold for the upper limit of the normal range (26,28,31).

There are many formulas available for the calculation of ovarian volume, and data regarding the degree of correlation between some of these formulas and true three-dimensional US volume measurements have been considered (32). Noting that any calculation of ovarian volume is, at best, an estimate, and citing the practical appeal of calculating ovarian volume using traditional, frequently cited methods, investigators decided that ovarian volume should be calculated on the basis of the simplified formula for a prolate ellipsoid (0.5 × length × width × thickness of the ovary) (Fig 4) (27,28,33–35). Strictly speaking, the ellipsoid defined with this formula is not prolate, since a prolate ellipsoid is defined mathematically as the shape created by rotating an ellipse around its long axis. Because the mathematic definition of a prolate ellipsoid requires that two of its three axes be equal, it is more accurate to refer to this formula as the simplified formula for an ellipsoid.

Number of Follicles.—The threshold for the number of follicles was based on performance data (receiving operator characteristic curves) assessing the ability of different thresholds for the number and size of follicles to help discriminate between healthy control subjects and PCOS patients. These data suggested that 12 or more follicles 2–9 mm in size per ovary provided the best threshold for the diagnosis of PCOS (36). Consequently, this threshold was adopted in place of all previous work that had attempted to define the polycystic ovary in terms of the number and size of follicles.

The adoption of this criterion for defining a polycystic ovary marks a shift in methodology from prior work, which attempted to define the polycystic ovary on the basis of its appearance in a single US imaging plane. This includes the often-cited definition of the polycystic ovary as one that contains (in one plane) at least 10 follicles arranged peripherally around an echodense stroma (26). The key technical requirement for the assessment of the number of follicles is that the number of antral follicles present throughout the entire volume of the ovary be counted (Fig 5).

Polycystic Ovaries in Patients without PCOS.—Women who are undergoing investigation for other gynecologic complaints may be found to have polycystic ovaries at US. Data suggest that 23% of women of reproductive age will have findings of polycystic ovaries (37). However, only 5%–10% of these women will have classic symptoms of PCOS such as infertility, amenorrhea, signs of hirsutism, or obesity (38).

The variations in ovarian volume and antral follicle counts by age have been studied. The overall trend of follicle count relative to age is biphasic, demonstrating a 4.8% annual decline before 37 years of age and a more rapid 11.7% annual decline thereafter (39). However, significant variation in follicle count is seen in particular age groups. In one study, antral follicle counts varied from seven to 22 during the early follicular phase of the menstrual cycle (day 2, 3, or 4) in women less than 30 years of age (39).

The variability in ovarian volume by age has also been studied. One large study measured the mean ovarian volume in 13,963 women undergoing screening for ovarian cancer using the nonsimplified formula for an ellipsoid (0.523 × length × width × thickness) (31). By defining abnormal ovarian volume as two standard deviations above the mean (the same convention as that used by the Rotterdam consensus group), the authors determined the upper limit of the normal range to be 20 cm3 for premenopausal women and 10 cm3 for postmenopausal women (31). However, such data in healthy patients are not directly comparable to data regarding ovarian volume in PCOS patients, since ovaries with dominant follicles or corpus luteal cysts were included in the “normal” data.

Stromal Echogenicity and Volume.—One of the earliest described characteristics of a polycystic ovary is an increase in stromal echogenicity (26). Although there have been many efforts to correlate qualitative indexes of stromal echogenicity with PCOS (27,40), the intrinsic echogenicity of the ovarian stroma is no different in PCOS than in the normal ovary; the subjective impression of increased stromal echogenicity is primarily due to the increase in stromal volume (41). It has been demonstrated that increased stromal volume correlates positively with serum androgen levels (42). However, no standardized method exists for determining stromal volume. Because overall ovarian volume correlates well with stromal volume in polycystic ovaries (42) and is more easily measured in clinical practice, the determination of overall ovarian volume is a reliable surrogate for ovarian stromal assessment.

Imaging and Reporting Considerations
Section:
prevnext

With regard to the imaging of polycystic ovaries, transvaginal US is preferred because it often provides optimal visualization of the internal structure of the ovary, particularly in obese patients. Data suggest that, compared with transvaginal US, the appearance of polycystic ovaries may not be detected at transabdominal US in up to 30% of women with PCOS (43). If transabdominal US is required, as may be the case in women who have never been sexually active or who decline transvaginal examination, care should be taken that the bladder is adequately filled but not overfilled, since an overfilled bladder can compress the ovary, potentially leading to a greater deviation from the model of an ellipsoid used to calculate ovarian volume (24). In addition, it may be helpful to record cine clips through each ovary to assist in counting follicles.

Regularly menstruating women should undergo scanning during the early follicular phase (days 3–5). Oligo- or amenorrheic women may be scanned at random, or between days 3 and 5 after progesterone-induced bleeding.
A history of oral contraceptive use should be elicited, since oral contraceptives cause a decrease in ovarian size, thereby decreasing the sensitivity of US evaluation. It should be noted that the Rotterdam definition of polycystic ovaries does not formally apply to women with such a history (24). Nevertheless, it has been shown that the polycystic appearance of the ovary persists despite the presence of oral contraceptives (44), so that the reporting of US findings such as follicle count and ovarian volume may be clinically helpful.

As mentioned earlier, women with PCOS are frequently ovulatory. The presence of a dominant follicle (defined as a follicle whose longitudinal, transverse, and anteroposterior diameters average more than 10 mm) (Fig 6a, 6b) or corpus luteum (Fig 6c) may increase the ovarian volume above the 10-cm3 threshold. Such a finding should prompt repeat scanning during the next menstrual cycle (Fig 6) (24). Similarly, the presence of asymmetry in ovarian volume should prompt further investigation for an underlying cause, such as an isoechoic cyst or corpus luteum (24). The range of follicular sizes, as well as the size of the largest follicle in each ovary as determined based on the average of measurements taken in three axes, should be evaluated and reported. Careful evaluation and reporting is important, since the presence of a single ovary fitting the Rotterdam definition is sufficient to diagnose polycystic ovaries (24). The Rotterdam definition is explicit in stating that women with polycystic ovaries but no evidence of ovulatory dysfunction or hyperandrogenism should not be considered to have PCOS. Given the significant number of women of reproductive age who have polycystic ovaries as defined on the basis of US criteria, it may be prudent to communicate this observation in the report, for example: “Findings meet the Rotterdam 2003 definition for polycystic ovaries. In the absence of ovulatory dysfunction or either clinically or biochemically diagnosed hyperandrogenism, findings are nonspecific and do not indicate the presence of polycystic ovarian syndrome.”

With respect to nonhirsute PCOS (the controversial phenotype defined by the Rotterdam criteria and later excluded by the Androgen Excess and PCOS Society guidelines), it may be necessary to note the controversy surrounding the inclusion of this group in the spectrum of PCOS. This is especially relevant for women with a history of ovulatory dysfunction (eg, oligo- or amenorrhea), since additional findings of polycystic ovaries satisfy the requirement for meeting two of the three Rotterdam criteria for diagnosis of the syndrome. (“Findings meet the Rotterdam 2003 definition for polycystic ovaries. Findings and the clinical history of ovulatory dysfunction in the absence of hyperandrogenism satisfy Rotterdam criteria, but not the Androgen Excess and PCOS Society guidelines, for the diagnosis of PCOS. Careful clinical evaluation is warranted.”)

Evaluation for PCOS in adolescent girls and postmenopausal women merits special consideration. Before the Rotterdam consensus statement was issued, the presence of polycystic ovaries was reported in postmenopausal women, and it was discovered that the presence of polycystic ovaries after menopause correlates with increased concentrations of serum testosterone and triglycerides (45). However, no firm threshold was identified by the Rotterdam consensus group for identification of polycystic ovaries after menopause in terms of ovarian volume and follicle count (24). What is known is that polycystic ovaries in postmenopausal women tend to have greater volume (6.4 cm3 versus 3.7 cm3) and demonstrate more follicles (9.0 versus 1.7) than normal postmenopausal ovaries (45).

With regard to adolescents, there are data that suggest that bilateral ovarian volumes greater than 10 cm3 correlate with the presence of PCOS in patients between 12 and 20 years of age if they have concurrent symptoms of menstrual irregularity (46). However, as in postmenopausal women, the Rotterdam consensus group identifies no specific threshold for identifying polycystic ovaries in adolescents. The consensus group also cites the difficulty in distinguishing a polycystic ovary from what has traditionally been referred to as a multicystic or multifollicular ovary, defined as an ovary in which there are six or more follicles, usually 4–10 mm in diameter, with normal stromal echogenicity and described as the common appearance of ovaries in adolescents (26). That being said, PCOS manifests itself around the time of menarche, and premature pubarche may be a harbinger of the syndrome (47). Because early intervention may ameliorate development of undesirable sequelae such as impaired glucose tolerance, the lack of a defined threshold in adolescents does not suggest that the reporting of ovarian volume and follicle count is without merit. Rather, it places more emphasis on careful evaluation of the clinical picture and endocrinologic abnormalities.

The use of magnetic resonance (MR) imaging for the assessment of polycystic ovaries has also been explored (48–50). According to the American College of Radiology Appropriateness Criteria, pelvic MR imaging is recommended in cases in which US is inconclusive or nondiagnostic. The use of MR imaging is generally not warranted in the evaluation of polycystic ovaries due to its increased cost and the typically adequate visualization of the ovaries at transvaginal US. However, MR imaging may be helpful in cases in which transvaginal US cannot be performed and transabdominal US does not offer adequate visualization (eg, in cases of extreme obesity).

The characteristic T2-weighted MR imaging appearance of a polycystic ovary is described as consisting of abundant hypointense central stroma with small peripheral T2-hyperintense cysts (Fig 7) (48). Although polycystic and normal ovaries appear to be distinguishable at MR imaging (49,50), the literature on MR imaging in PCOS is scant, and no consensus has been reached as to an MR imaging definition for a polycystic ovary. Recent work applying the Rotterdam US definition to MR imaging has demonstrated differences in ovarian volume and follicle count between women with PCOS and healthy control subjects (50). However, as in prior studies (49), the authors noted an overlapping of findings in these two populations (50). This is similar to what is seen at US assessment and is not surprising, given the frequency with which polycystic ovaries are seen in asymptomatic individuals. Until more is known about the performance of MR imaging versus US, it may be practical to report follicle counts and ovarian volume if MR imaging is performed for the assessment of polycystic ovaries.

Clinical Evaluation of Patients with Documented PCOS
Section:
prevnext

Current recommendations are to screen women with a diagnosis of PCOS for type 2 diabetes and impaired glucose tolerance on the basis of fasting glucose level and results of a 2-hour glucose tolerance test. These women should also be screened for cardiovascular risk factors on the basis of body mass index, fasting triglyceride and lipoprotein levels, and other metabolic syndrome risk factors (22).

The mainstays of treatment for PCOS are lifestyle modification and pharmacologic therapy. Reduction in body weight improves menstrual regularity, spontaneous pregnancy rates, and symptoms of hirsutism, as well as glucose tolerance and hyperlipidemia (22). Such improvements have been shown with weight loss of as little as 5% (51).

Pharmacologic treatment of hirsutism in PCOS aims to reduce androgen levels, decrease androgen production, augment androgen binding to SHBG, and block androgen action at the level of the target tissue. Combined estrogen-progesterone oral contraceptives are the most frequently used pharmacologic treatment. These contraceptives act by a multitude of mechanisms, including suppression of pituitary LH secretion (and, hence, of androgen production), direct suppression of ovarian androgen synthesis, and stimulation of SHBG production in the liver (thereby decreasing free testosterone). Antiandrogens such as spironolactone are also used in the treatment of hirsutism in PCOS.

The administration of combined oral contraceptives also has the benefit of offering endometrial protection. Alternatively, cyclic progesterone or progestin-containing intrauterine devices (IUDs) may be used with the intent of reducing the risk of endometrial cancer (Fig 9) (22). Endometrial biopsy may be considered in patients with chronic anovulation. Some investigators advocate the use of US to measure endometrial thickness in deciding whether to perform endometrial biopsy (52). The appearance of the endometrium may be important for endometrial assessment in PCOS. Specifically, a heterogeneous appearance or cystic changes within the endometrium may be more suggestive of endometrial hyperplasia (53).

Insulin-sensitizing agents, most commonly metformin, may also be used, especially in patients with impaired glucose tolerance. These agents have the additional benefit of decreasing circulating androgen levels and increasing ovulation rates (4,54). Although metformin appears to influence ovarian steroidogenesis directly, this is not thought to be the main reason for the attenuation of ovarian androgen production in women with PCOS. Rather, metformin inhibits the output of hepatic glucose, compelling a lower serum insulin concentration and thereby reducing the androgen production of theca cells.

Ovulation induction agents are used to treat infertility. Oral agents such as clomiphene citrate are initially administered; treatment with injectable gonadotropins is reserved for patients who are resistant to or fail to respond to oral therapy. US may have an additional role in the management of PCOS in these patients. There is some evidence that ovarian volume, in addition to other factors such as body mass index, ovulatory cycle history, and serum androgen levels, may be helpful in predicting fertility outcomes in response to treatment with clomiphene citrate (55). Similarly, although not pertaining exclusively to patients with PCOS, the presence of polycystic ovaries may be a risk factor for ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome in women who are undergoing ovarian stimulation for in vitro fertilization (18,56).

Conclusions
Section:
prevnext

Ovarian imaging is crucial in the evaluation of patients with suspected PCOS. The imaging report should be specific and should include ovarian volumes and antral follicle counts, in addition to pertinent findings such as the presence of a dominant follicle or corpus luteum. Although findings of polycystic ovaries at routine US are common and are frequently not associated with PCOS, awareness of the criteria and definitions used to diagnose PCOS is important, particularly in patients who are referred for evaluation of ovulatory dysfunction or hyperandrogenism. Thus, to participate in workup, radiologists must have a working knowledge of the clinical and imaging criteria for PCOS.
